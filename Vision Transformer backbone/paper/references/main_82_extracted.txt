Abstract
Clinical vision models must identify rare malignant cases
while remaining calibrated, computationally efficient, and
aware of uncertainty. Existing softmax classifiers are of-
ten overconfident under severe class imbalance, whereas
ensemble-based uncertainty methods increase inference
cost and conventional sparse training ignores reliability
signals. We propose the Universal MDEP architecture,
an uncertainty-guided dynamic sparse training frame-
work. In this report, Swin-MDEP denotes the Vision
Transformer instantiation of MDEP that uses a Swin
Transformer backbone. Swin-MDEP replaces softmax
outputs with Dirichlet evidential predictions, enforces
NVIDIA 2:4 structured sparsity, and updates sparse topol-
ogy through cooperative Microglia–Astrocyte agents that
use aleatoric and epistemic uncertainty gradients. A mor-
phological cache further reduces repeated mask-sorting
overhead during training. On ISIC 2024 skin-lesion clas-
sification, Swin-MDEP achieves a balanced clinical op-
erating point with AUROC 0.8852, sensitivity 0.7342,
specificity 0.9131, and ECE 0.0479, outperforming sev-
eral uncertainty-aware and sparse evidential baselines in
calibration and selective prediction. These results sug-
gest that evidential uncertainty can serve not only as an
output diagnostic signal but also as a structural signal
for efficient and safer medical AI.
Introduction
The rapid development of large-scale deep learning mod-
els has established new benchmarks of accuracy across
many computer vision tasks and complex data analyses.
Architectures such as Convolutional Neural Networks
(CNNs) [38] and Vision Transformers (ViTs) [9] currently
dominate image recognition benchmarks. However, when
these models are transitioned from laboratory settings to
demanding real-world deployment systems—such as medi-
cal diagnosis [41], autonomous driving safety support [53],
and resource-constrained edge devices [52]—they reveal
three core systemic weaknesses [28]:
1. Enormous computational cost: The power con-
sumption and memory footprint of dense models ex-
ceed the hardware constraints of many embedded sys-
tems.
2. Overconfident predictions: Standard deep mod-
els tend to make highly overconfident predictions
even when the input data is heavily noisy, out-of-
distribution (OOD), or represents out-of-domain sam-
ples [29].
3. Lack of uncertainty quantification: They lack the
ability to quantify predictive uncertainty in a mathe-
matically rigorous and transparent manner, rendering
risk-sensitive decision-making impossible.
Limitations of Traditional Softmax
Most traditional neural networks deploy a softmax acti-
vation function in their final classification layer to map
raw logit values to a probability distribution [29, 31].
Although softmax is highly effective at squeezing output
values into the interval [0, 1], its geometric nature is in-
herently sharp and often pushes predicted probabilities
close to the extreme bounds. This creates a false sense of
security, causing the network to assign a 99% probability
to a prediction even when it is essentially guessing ran-
domly on completely unfamiliar data [25]. This critical
blind spot in decision-making, particularly when facing
highly imbalanced datasets [36], leads to catastrophic
risks in clinical settings [42].
Evidential Deep Learning (EDL)
To fundamentally resolve the overconfidence issue, Evi-
dential Deep Learning (EDL) [1, 5] has been developed
as a revolutionary framework. EDL extends standard
parametric deep learning approaches to higher-order dis-
tributions, allowing the model to directly predict the
parameters of a Dirichlet distribution instead of out-
putting deterministic point probabilities [3, 4, 6]. In this
way, a single EDL model can deliver uncertainty estima-
tion performance comparable to computationally heavy
methods such as Deep Ensembles [25] or Monte Carlo
Dropout (MC Dropout) [24], while stably quantifying
both key uncertainty sources: epistemic (model lack of
knowledge) and aleatoric (inherent data noise) uncertain-
ties [27], despite ongoing debates regarding its practical
1
calibration capability [7].
Dynamic Sparse Training (DST)
In parallel with breakthroughs in uncertainty estimation,
Dynamic Sparse Training (DST) [11, 12] has emerged as
a leading paradigm for addressing computational chal-
lenges. Rather than training a massive dense network to
completion and then pruning it post-hoc [14, 13], DST
dynamically optimizes a sparse network topology from
scratch during the training phase by continuously pruning
weak connections and regrowing new ones in promising
regions [15]. While DST provides immense benefits for
hardware efficiency, most current pruning/growth crite-
ria rely solely on weight magnitude or task gradients,
remaining entirely blind to reliability and uncertainty.
The Birth of MDEP
The Microglial-Driven Evidential Pruning (MDEP) ar-
chitecture was conceived to fill this mathematical and
structural gap. By unifying EDL and DST under a biolog-
ically inspired framework, MDEP establishes a universal
framework that allows computation graphs to not only
sparsify for hardware acceleration but also reshape their
internal topology based on the network’s evidential flow
and uncertainty signals. In this terminology, Universal
MDEP refers to the general architecture and training
principle, whereas Swin-MDEP is the concrete imple-
mentation that applies MDEP to a Swin Transformer
backbone for vision tasks.
Main Contributions.
The main contributions of this
paper are:
• Swin-MDEP Framework: A dynamic sparse train-
ing architecture for Vision Transformers that integrates
Dirichlet evidential learning with NVIDIA 2:4 struc-
tured sparsity.
• Multi-Agent Dynamics: A cooperative Microglia–
Astrocyte mechanism that leverages the gradients of
epistemic and aleatoric uncertainties to guide connec-
tion pruning and regrowth.
• Implementation Optimization: A morphological
cache mechanism that stores and reuses masks and
local survival thresholds within each epoch, removing
redundant sorting operations.
• Clinical Evaluation: Extensive experiments on the
ISIC 2024 dataset demonstrating an optimal balance
between clinical sensitivity, specificity, expected cali-
bration error (ECE), and selective classification.
Related Work
Calibration and Uncertainty Estimation
Calibration studies indicate that modern neural net-
works are frequently overconfident despite achieving
high accuracy [29, 31]. Deep Ensembles [25] and MC
Dropout [24] improve uncertainty representation but sig-
nificantly increase inference latency. Evidential Deep
Learning (EDL) [1] directly models the Dirichlet distribu-
tion, yet it still requires mechanisms to suppress spurious
evidence and achieve reliable empirical calibration [7].
Sparse Training and Network Pruning
Post-training pruning [14] and the Lottery Ticket Hypoth-
esis [13] reduce parameters but generally require full dense
pre-training. Dynamic Sparse Training (DST) [12, 11]
updates the active network topology on the fly during
training. However, existing pruning/growth criteria are
mostly based on weight magnitude or task-specific gradi-
ents, leaving Dirichlet uncertainty untapped as a struc-
tural signal.
Medical AI on Imbalanced Data
Clinical skin cancer screening systems face extremely
low prevalence rates of malignant lesions, where high
overall accuracy or specificity can mask critically low
sensitivity [41, 36]. Swin-MDEP differs from these base-
lines by simultaneously optimizing minority-class sensi-
tivity, probability calibration, selective prediction, and
hardware-level structured sparsity.
Background and Preliminaries
The MDEP system completely replaces the traditional
softmax activation function at the classification layer and
adopts mathematical foundations derived from Subjective
Logic (SL) [2]. This theoretical framework formalizes
the relationship between ”evidence” gathered from data
and the ”belief” of the model, mapping the network’s
outputs directly to the concentration parameters of a
Dirichlet distribution [1]. The Dirichlet distribution acts
as a conjugate prior for the multinomial distribution,
providing a deeper and more mathematically rigorous
probabilistic interpretation.

Morphological Cache Optimization.
In the naive
implementation, the discrete mask M and local survival
thresholds τ are recomputed via sorting (torch.sort)
and search (torch.topk) operations over all weights in
every forward pass of every mini-batch. Since the latent
scores S are updated only once at the beginning of each
epoch (at batch 0), performing these sorting operations
on tens of millions of parameters at every mini-batch
step is redundant and introduces a severe performance
bottleneck.
To resolve this design flaw, Swin-MDEP introduces
the Morphological Cache mechanism. The threshold τ is
registered as a non-persistent buffer (persistent=False)
inside the MDEPLinear and MDEPConv2d layers. Both the
binary mask M and thresholds τ are calculated once
at the end of the warmup phase or when updating the
network structure at the start of each epoch; they are
then stored and reused directly across all remaining mini-
batches of that epoch. Setting persistent=False avoids
checkpoint pollution, maintaining backward compatibility
with pre-trained weights.
This optimization removes
redundant sorting operations, lowers CPU/GPU resource
overhead, and significantly accelerates the forward pass.

connection topology updates, driven by two agents in-

compression algorithms simply prune weights with the

computation graph by balancing task representation re-
quirements against the spread of uncertainty.

Dirichlet KL Knowledge Distillation
To stabilize the sparse Swin-T student network during its
transition to 2:4 structured sparsity, the system applies
knowledge distillation techniques [21, 22], specifically evi-
dential distribution distillation [23] from a fully converged
dense ResNet-18 MDEP teacher model [38].
Remark 5 (Why ResNet-18 instead of ResNet-50 as
Teacher?). Three main reasons justify this selection:
(i) Cross-Architecture Inductive Bias: ResNet-18
is a pure CNN with strong local spatial inductive bi-
ases. Distilling from a CNN to a ViT helps transfer
spatial locality to Swin-T’s Self-Attention mecha-
nism, focusing attention on lesion regions.
(ii) Preventing Overfitting on Rare Minority
Classes: A ResNet-50 teacher is highly complex
(50 layers with bottleneck blocks) and prone to mem-
orizing the small number of malignant samples
(∼390), which distorts its Dirichlet uncertainty
estimation (αT ). ResNet-18 is simpler and acts
as a regularizer, providing better-calibrated teacher
distributions.
(iii) VRAM Optimization: Hosting both the teacher
and student networks in GPU memory alongside
the MDEP engine during training is memory-
intensive. ResNet-18 (∼11.7M parameters) avoids
Out-of-Memory (OOM) errors on standard GPUs
(e.g., T4 with 15–16GB VRAM), whereas ResNet-
50 (∼25.6M parameters) easily triggers VRAM
overflow.

Three-Fold Role of Knowledge Distillation:
1. Sensitivity Improvement: The teacher provides

separating ambiguous boundaries.
2. Better Uncertainty Calibration: The distillation

Experiments and Evaluation
Experimental Setup
Dataset: ISIC 2024
The performance of MDEP is evaluated on the clinical
ISIC 2024 (Skin Cancer Detection with 3D-TBP)
dataset [40, 44, 43]. The extreme class imbalance (malig-
nant cases account for only 0.15% of the samples, a ratio
of 1:670) represents a challenging optimization landscape.
Data Processing and Training Set Balancing:
• Stratified Split: Split the data into 80/20 train/test
sets using a fixed seed (random state=42).
• Under-sampling: To balance the training set, we
keep all malignant samples and randomly sample be-
nign samples at a ratio of 1 : 20. This under-sampling
reduces the training set from over 400,000 to around
8,000 samples, accelerating training by more than 50×
while retaining all rare positive cases. The test set dis-
tribution is left untouched to represent clinical reality.
• Data Augmentation: Horizontal and vertical flips
are applied to the training set; test samples are only
resized and normalized.
• ImageNet Normalization: Normalization uses µ =
(0.485, 0.456, 0.406) and σ = (0.229, 0.224, 0.225).
• Input Dimension: Images are resized to 224 × 224
pixels.
Training Protocol and Hyperparameters
Precision Arithmetic (Mixed Precision):
The
backbone runs using AMP 16-bit mixed precision [51].
Evidential Focal Loss and Dirichlet math functions are
cast to FP32 to prevent arithmetic underflow in ln(·),
Γ(·), and ψ(·) calls.

Model performance is evaluated across three categories

• Classification Performance:

Main Results
MDEP’s performance is analyzed across three groups of
metrics: classification capacity, probability calibration,
and selective prediction.
Classification Performance and Clinical Bound-
aries
Collapse of Static Architectures:
Traditional deep
learning models trained with Softmax Cross-Entropy suf-
fer from representation collapse (Mode Collapse) under
severe class imbalance: ResNet-50 [38] and ViT-B/16 [9]
achieve high Specificity (0.99+) but extremely poor Sen-
sitivity (0.225 and 0.000, respectively). ViT completely
fails to identify malignant cases. Bayesian methods (Deep
Ensembles [25], MC Dropout [24]) improve representation
but still return low Sensitivity (0.31–0.35).
MDEP Balance Solution:
MDEP automatically
sweeps the probability space [0.01, 0.99] to find the thresh-
old that maximizes Balanced Accuracy, yielding: Sen-
sitivity = 0.7342, Specificity = 0.9131, Macro-AUROC
= 0.8852.
Ablation Study
Ablation results verify the contribution of the two struc-
tural agents:
• Prune Only (No Astrocyte): Structural capacity
is damaged; Specificity drops to 0.7466, and Macro-
AUROC falls to 0.8025.
• Grow Only (No Microglia): Lacking the synapse
clearing agent, the network retains noisy connections
and experiences severe overconfidence.
Calibration and Risk Quantification
The full Swin-MDEP model maintains a low ECE [30]
(0.0479), demonstrating reliable uncertainty quantifica-
tion despite criticisms of EDL’s calibration limits [7]. The
combination of the Dirichlet KL penalty (which shrinks
spurious evidence) and the Microglia agent (which prunes
noise-absorbing connections) yields a smooth Dirichlet
probability simplex. In comparison, EfficientNet-B4 [39]
reaches a high ECE of 0.3420 (”Calibration Crisis”), while
Posterior Networks [26] and EF-DST experience ”False
Positive Collapse” (Sensitivity 1.0 but Specificity 0.0,
ECE ≈0.5).

Qualitative and Error Analysis
Qualitatively, samples displaying low Dirichlet evidence
or high epistemic uncertainty generally correspond to
rare lesions, heavily noisy images, or ambiguous clinical
presentations. Instead of forcing the model to make an
overconfident softmax prediction, Swin-MDEP utilizes
these uncertainty signals to defer difficult cases to clinical
experts. The remaining diagnostic errors mostly occur
when malignant features are obscured by image noise
(such as hair, shadows, or reflective glare) or when early-
stage malignant lesions display visual patterns highly
similar to benign lesions, suggesting the benefit of incor-
porating patient clinical metadata in future iterations.
Conclusion
Swin-MDEP provides a mathematically sound, cali-
brated, and hardware-efficient deep learning framework
for clinical melanoma screening. By adapting the Astro-
cyte’s multidimensional gradient-pooling mechanism to
Shifted-Window Attention backbones [8], using Smoothed
STE [20] for 2:4 structured sparsity optimization [16],
and implementing cosine-decay Dirichlet distillation [21]
from a dense ResNet-18 MDEP teacher [38], the model
achieves robust performance under severe class imbal-
ance.
The main contributions include:
1. Theoretical Contribution: A biologically inspired
opposing-force mechanism that uses Dirichlet uncer-
tainty gradients to guide connection pruning and re-
growth.
2. Technical Contribution: Successful integration of
dynamic NVIDIA 2:4 structured sparsity during evi-
dential learning on Vision Transformers, compatible
with GPU Tensor Cores.
3. Application Contribution: An integrated diagnos-
tic safeguard, clinical calibration, and prediction de-
ferral (Selective Classification) for safe deployment in
clinical decision support.
Limitations and Future Work
The current study focuses on the ISIC 2024 dataset and
requires validation across multiple clinics, imaging de-
vices, and patient populations. Future directions include
prospective clinical evaluations, incorporating patient
clinical metadata, evaluating subgroup fairness, and ex-
tending the Swin-MDEP framework to image segmenta-
tion or multimodal clinical diagnosis.
Ethical Statement
The Swin-MDEP architecture is designed as a clinical
decision support system and is not intended to replace
professional clinical judgment. Because medical image
datasets may contain biases related to imaging hardware,
skin tone representation, geographic clinics, and image
collection protocols, any clinical deployment must un-
dergo rigorous assessments of fairness, calibration, and
failure detection on the target patient cohort. The selec-
tive classification mechanism reduces risks by deferring
high-uncertainty predictions to experts, but it does not
completely eliminate false negatives or false positives.
Patient data usage must comply with medical data pro-
tection standards, ensuring anonymization and access
control. Furthermore, post-deployment monitoring is rec-
ommended to identify data drift and performance decay.
